03 — The Turning Point

WTF Happened in 1971?

August 15, 1971. Nixon went on live television and severed the dollar from gold. The promise was broken. For the first time in history: money backed by nothing.

The problem? Countries like France were calling America's bluff — demanding gold for their dollars. The U.S. had printed far more dollars than it had gold to back them. Nixon didn't close the gold window to protect the people. He closed it to protect the government.

Every economic chart breaks at 1971. Debt explodes. Wages flatline. Housing, education, healthcare — all parabolic. Unsound money corrupts everything it touches.

The dollar's value was re-anchored — not to gold, but to oil and military power
03b — The Deal

The Petrodollar

1971 created a crisis: if the dollar wasn't backed by gold, why would the world keep using it? Nixon and Kissinger had an answer.

In 1974, they struck a deal with Saudi Arabia. The terms were simple: Saudi Arabia prices all oil sales worldwide in U.S. dollars and reinvests its surplus oil revenues into U.S. Treasury bonds. In return, the U.S. provides full military protection to the Saudi kingdom.

Every OPEC nation followed. Overnight, every country on Earth needed dollars — not because the dollar was sound, but because you couldn't buy oil without it. The dollar went from being backed by gold to being backed by oil and aircraft carriers.

This is the Petrodollar system. It gave the U.S. the "exorbitant privilege" of printing the world's reserve currency — funding endless deficits, endless wars, and endless debt. The entire global economy was forced onto a currency controlled by one government.

"The dollar is no longer backed by gold. It's backed by the U.S. military and the implicit threat that if you try to price oil in anything else, you'll be visited by that military."
— A reality of the post-1971 world

04 — The Cause

How the 2008 Crisis Happened

After the dot-com crash, the Fed slashed interest rates to 1% — flooding the economy with cheap money. Banks went on a lending spree.

They gave mortgages to borrowers with no income, no job, no assets — called NINJA loans. By 2006, subprime mortgages made up 24% of all new mortgages (up from 8% in 2003). Delinquency rates would hit 10.4% — one in ten American mortgages not being paid.

Wall Street bundled these toxic mortgages into products called CDOs (Collateralized Debt Obligations) and sold them worldwide. Rating agencies like Moody's and S&P gave them AAA ratings — the same rating as U.S. Treasury bonds. Pension funds, cities, and foreign banks bought billions in junk debt they were told was safe.

When housing prices fell 33% from peak, the house of cards collapsed. Lehman Brothers — a 158-year-old bank — went bankrupt overnight. The Dow dropped 54%. $11 trillion in household wealth vanished. 8.7 million Americans lost their jobs.

Citizens lost homes and savings. Banks got bailouts and bonuses.
04b — The Response

Bailouts, Bonuses, and the Money Printer

The government's answer: $700 billion in taxpayer-funded bailouts (TARP). The banks that caused the crisis were deemed "too big to fail." AIG alone received $182 billion.

In 2009, bailed-out banks paid $32.6 billion in bonuses to executives — while 3.8 million Americans faced foreclosure that same year. No major bank executive went to prison. One system of rules for them. Another for everyone else.

Then the Fed launched quantitative easing — printing $4.5 trillion between 2008-2014, expanding its balance sheet from $900 billion to $4.5 trillion. The money didn't go to Main Street. It inflated asset prices, making the wealthy wealthier and leaving working Americans behind.
