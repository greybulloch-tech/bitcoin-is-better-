03 — The Turning Point

WTF Happened in 1971?

On August 15, 1971, President Nixon went on live TV and announced that the dollar would no longer be backed by gold. From that moment on, the U.S. dollar was backed by absolutely nothing.

Why did he do it? The U.S. had been printing way more dollars than it had gold to cover. Other countries noticed and started asking for their gold back. Instead of fixing the problem, Nixon just changed the rules.

Look at any chart — debt, housing costs, wages, inequality — they all go sideways or explode right around 1971. When you break the money, you break everything.

03b — The Deal

The Petrodollar

After 1971, people had a fair question: if the dollar isn't backed by gold anymore, why would the rest of the world keep using it?

The answer: oil. In 1974, the U.S. made a deal with Saudi Arabia. Saudi Arabia would sell all its oil in U.S. dollars only. In return, the U.S. would protect Saudi Arabia with its military. Every other oil-producing country followed.

This meant every country on Earth had to hold U.S. dollars just to buy oil — not because the dollar was good money, but because you literally couldn't buy energy without it.

This is called the Petrodollar system. It let the U.S. keep printing money and running up debt, because the whole world was forced to use dollars. One country's government got to control the money everyone else depends on.

"The dollar isn't backed by gold anymore. It's backed by the U.S. military — and the understanding that if you try to sell oil in a different currency, that military might show up."
— The reality since 1971
04 — The Cause

How the 2008 Crisis Happened

After the dot-com bust, the government made it super cheap to borrow money. Banks took advantage and started giving out home loans to basically anyone — even people with no income, no job, and no savings. These were called "NINJA loans."

By 2006, nearly 1 in 4 new home loans were going to people who probably couldn't pay them back. Banks didn't care — they bundled these bad loans into packages and sold them to investors around the world. Rating agencies said these packages were safe investments. They weren't.

When home prices dropped 33%, the whole thing fell apart. A 158-year-old bank (Lehman Brothers) went bankrupt overnight. The stock market lost over half its value. $11 trillion in regular people's wealth disappeared. 8.7 million Americans lost their jobs.

04b — The Response

Bailouts, Bonuses, and the Money Printer

The banks that caused the crash? The government bailed them out with $700 billion of taxpayer money. They called the banks "too big to fail." One company alone — AIG — got $182 billion.

Here's the crazy part: in 2009, those same bailed-out banks paid their executives $32.6 billion in bonuses — while 3.8 million regular Americans were losing their homes. Not one major bank CEO went to jail. Different rules for the rich.

Then the government's central bank (the Fed) printed $4.5 trillion in new money between 2008 and 2014. That money didn't help working people — it pumped up stock prices and real estate, making the rich richer and everyone else poorer.

"The Times 03/Jan/2009 Chancellor on brink of second bailout for banks" — Bitcoin's creator put this newspaper headline into the very first Bitcoin block ever made. It's a permanent reminder of why Bitcoin exists.
05 — The Creator

The Mystery of Satoshi Nakamoto

In October 2008, someone using the name "Satoshi Nakamoto" published a 9-page document describing a new kind of money. In January 2009, the first Bitcoin was created.

Satoshi worked on it quietly for about two years. Then they sent one last message — "I've moved on to other things" — and disappeared forever. They left behind about 1 million bitcoin that has never been touched.

To this day, nobody knows who Satoshi is. There's no CEO, no company, no office. Bitcoin doesn't belong to anyone — it belongs to everyone.

06 — The Technology

How the Blockchain Works

Think of the blockchain as a giant shared spreadsheet that lives on thousands of computers around the world. Every 10 minutes, a new page of transactions gets added. Nobody can change or delete old pages.

Special computers called "miners" use energy to verify transactions and keep the network secure. This is what makes Bitcoin trustworthy — it costs real energy to create, so nobody can fake it.

If you hold your own keys (basically your password), nobody — no bank, no government — can take your bitcoin. Your keys, your money. No keys, not your money.

07 — The Thesis

The Case for Sound Money

When money is good, society gets better. That's the big idea behind the book The Bitcoin Standard.

When money held its value (like under the gold standard), people saved, invested, and built things that lasted. When governments can print money whenever they want, it rewards borrowing and spending — and punishes the people who try to save.

Bitcoin has everything that made gold great (it's scarce and can't be faked), plus it's digital — so you can send it anywhere, instantly.

08 — 21 Million

Digital Scarcity

There will only ever be 21 million bitcoin. That number is baked into the code and can never be changed. No one can print more.

Every 4 years, the amount of new bitcoin created gets cut in half — so less and less new bitcoin enters the system over time. The last bitcoin won't be mined until around 2140.

This makes bitcoin even more scarce than gold. Gold miners keep finding more gold, but no one can ever make more bitcoin.
